<h2>Understanding the First and Second Laws of Thermodynamics</h2>

<h3>The First Law: Energy Conservation in Heat Engines</h3>

<p>\[\Delta U = Q + W\]</p>

<p>Where :</p>

<ul>

<li>\(\Delta U\): change in internal energy</li>

<li>\(Q\): heat added to the system</li>

<li>\(W\): work done by the system</li>

</ul>



<h3>The Second Law: Entropy and Limitations</h3>

<p>\[\Delta S_{\text{universe}} \geq 0\]</p>

<p>In a heat engine :</p>

<ul>

<li>Heat enters from a hot source</li>

<li>Some is converted to work</li>

<li>The rest is expelled to a cold sink</li>

</ul>

<p>This unavoidable loss limits the maximum efficiency of any real engine.</p>



<h2>The Carnot Engine: Theoretical Efficiency Limit</h2>

<p>The Carnot cycle is the ideal, reversible heat engine. Its efficiency is given by : </p>

<p>\[\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}\]</p>

<p>Where :</p>



<ul>



<li>\([\eta_{\text{Carnot}}\)  : is the efficiency</li>



<li>\(T_C \) : is the temperature of the cold reservoir (in Kelvin)</li>



<li>\(T_H \) : is the temperature of the hot reservoir (in Kelvin)</li>



</ul>





<img src="assets/images/carnot-cycle.png" alt="Carnot Cycle" width="500" />

<p>This equation defines the upper limit â€” no real engine can be more efficient due to irreversibilities.</p>

<h2>Entropy Flow in the Carnot Cycle</h2>

<p>In a Carnot engine :</p>

<ul>

<li>\(Q_H \) is the heat absorbed from the hot source</li>

<li>\(Q_C\) is heat rejected to the cold sink </li>

</ul>

<p>Work done is :</p>

<p><b>W = Q_H â€“ Q_C = \[Delta S Ã— (T_H â€“ T_C)\] </b></p>

<p>Entropy is conserved in this ideal case : thereâ€™s no net generation.</p>



<h2>Otto Cycle: Modeling the Gasoline Engine</h2>

<h3>Process Overview</h3>

<ol>

<li>Adiabatic compression</li>

<li>Isochoric (constant volume) heat addition</li>

<li>Adiabatic expansion</li>

<li>Isochoric heat rejection</li>

</ol>

<h3>Efficiency of the Otto Cycle</h3>

<p>\[\eta_{\text{Otto}} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma - 1}}\]</p>

<p>Where :</p>

<ul>

<li>\(r\) : compression ratio</li>

<li>\(\gamma\) : heat capacity ratio \((C_p/C_v)\)</li>

</ul>

<h3>Efficiency vs Compression Ratio</h3>

<p>Plotting efficiency against compression ratio reveals diminishing returns â€” after a certain point, increasing compression yields minimal gains due to knocking limits.</p>

<h2>Diesel Cycle: Heat Addition at Constant Pressure</h2>

<h3>Diesel Efficiency Equation</h3>

<p>\[ \eta_{\text{Diesel}} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma - 1}} \cdot

\left(\frac{\rho^{\gamma - 1}}{\gamma(\rho - 1)}\right) \]</p>

<p>Where :</p>

<ul>

<li>\(\rho\) : cut-off ratio \((V_3 / V_2)\)</li>

</ul>

<img src="assets/images/diesel-cycle.png" alt="Diesel Cycle" width="500" />

<p>Diesel engines allow higher compression ratios without knocking, making them more efficient in heavy-duty applications.</p>
