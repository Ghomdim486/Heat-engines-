Understanding the First and Second Laws of Thermodynamics

The First Law: Energy Conservation in Heat Engines

\[\Delta U = Q + W\]

Where :

  • \(\Delta U\): change in internal energy
  • \(Q\): heat added to the system
  • \(W\): work done by the system

The Second Law: Entropy and Limitations

\[\Delta S_{\text{universe}} \geq 0\]

In a heat engine :

  • Heat enters from a hot source
  • Some is converted to work
  • The rest is expelled to a cold sink

This unavoidable loss limits the maximum efficiency of any real engine.

The Carnot Engine: Theoretical Efficiency Limit

The Carnot cycle is the ideal, reversible heat engine. Its efficiency is given by :

\[\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}\]

Where :

  • \(\eta_{\text{Carnot}}\) : is the efficiency
  • \(T_C \) : is the temperature of the cold reservoir (in Kelvin)
  • \(T_H \) : is the temperature of the hot reservoir (in Kelvin)

This equation defines the upper limit â€” no real engine can be more efficient due to irreversibilities.

Entropy Flow in the Carnot Cycle

In a Carnot engine :

  • \(Q_H \) is the heat absorbed from the hot source
  • \(Q_C\) is heat rejected to the cold sink

Work done is :

\[W = Q_H - Q_C = \Delta S \cdot (T_H - T_C)\]

Entropy is conserved in this ideal case : thereâ€™s no net generation.

Otto Cycle: Modeling the Gasoline Engine

Process Overview

  1. Adiabatic compression
  2. Isochoric (constant volume) heat addition
  3. Adiabatic expansion
  4. Isochoric heat rejection

Efficiency of the Otto Cycle

\[\eta_{\text{Otto}} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma - 1}}\]

Where :

  • \(r\) : compression ratio
  • \(\gamma\) : heat capacity ratio \((C_p/C_v)\)

Efficiency vs Compression Ratio

Plotting efficiency against compression ratio reveals diminishing returns â€” after a certain point, increasing compression yields minimal gains due to knocking limits.

Diesel Cycle: Heat Addition at Constant Pressure

Diesel Efficiency Equation

\[ \eta_{\text{Diesel}} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma - 1}} \cdot \left(\frac{\rho^{\gamma - 1}}{\gamma(\rho - 1)}\right) \]

Where :

  • \(\rho\) : cut-off ratio \((V_3 / V_2)\)

Diesel engines allow higher compression ratios without knocking, making them more efficient in heavy-duty applications.

Symbolic vs Numeric Computation of Work

Symbolic (SymPy)

\[W = - \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P(V) \, dV\]

For adiabatic processes : \[P(V) = P_1 \cdot \left(\frac{V_1}{V}\right)^\gamma\]

Numeric (NumPy/Scipy)

  • Use discrete P-V data from simulation
  • Apply np.trapz() or scipy.integrate.simps()
  • Compare to ideal values
